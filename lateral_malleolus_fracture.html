DefinitionFracture of the distal fibula at or near the ankle joint; the lateral malleolus is involved in about 90% of ankle fractures.

MechanismRotational or inversion injury of the loaded foot, most often supination with external rotation.

Diagnosis

  • Focal bony tenderness over the lateral malleolus, swelling, ecchymosis.
  • Tenderness over the malleolus rather than the lateral ligaments distinguishes fracture from sprain.
  • Inability to bear weight for four steps.
  • Examine the medial side: medial tenderness or swelling implies deltoid injury and instability.
  • Palpate the proximal fibula and check distal pulses, sensation, and capillary refill.

Imaging

ViewsAP, lateral, mortise

  • keyFracture of distal fibula; assess level relative to syndesmosis for Weber classification
  • Medial clear space > superior clear space or lateral talar shift (indicates instability)
  • Decreased tibiofibular overlap or widened tibiofibular clear space (syndesmotic injury)
  • Medial clear space widening on weight-bearing or stress views (detects occult instability)

Key radiographic lines

  • Talocrural angle (about 83 plus or minus 4 degrees; loss indicates fibular shortening)
  • Medial clear space (should equal the superior clear space)
  • Talar tilt (medial to lateral superior clear space difference under 2 mm)
  • Tibiofibular clear space and overlap

Criteria normal vs acceptable vs refer

Isolated Weber B: stable vs unstablencbi, aafp
  • Stability of an isolated Weber B fibula fracture turns on whether the deltoid ligament and medial structures are intact, because they hold the talus under the tibia.
  • Competent deltoid (no medial tenderness or swelling, medial clear space equal to the superior clear space, no talar shift): stable, treat in a below-knee cast or boot and weightbear.
  • Incompetent deltoid (medial tenderness, medial clear space widening, or talar shift): functionally bimalleolar and unstable, refer for ORIF.
  • When the non-weightbearing mortise looks congruent but the deltoid is in question, stress the joint: a weightbearing AP or mortise view is preferred (physiological load), and a gravity-stress lateral-decubitus view is an alternative when the patient cannot bear weight.
  • Medial clear space over 5 mm on stress is highly suggestive of deltoid incompetence and instability; correlate clinically.
  • Syndesmotic instability shows as loss of tibiofibular overlap or tibiofibular clear space over 6 mm, confirmed with weight-bearing or external-rotation stress views when plain films are equivocal.
  • A manual external-rotation stress test is operator-dependent and less reliable than a weightbearing view; prefer weightbearing where feasible.
Ottawa rules

Ankle x-ray if malleolar-zone pain plus bony tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of either malleolus, or inability to bear weight four steps both immediately and in the ED. Foot x-ray if midfoot pain plus navicular or fifth-metatarsal-base tenderness, or the same inability to bear weight. Near 100% sensitive for clinically significant fractures.

Classification

Weber classification

TypeCharacteristicsTreatment
Weber ABelow the syndesmosis; transverse avulsion; syndesmosis and deltoid intact.Stable; below-knee cast or boot, weightbear.
Weber BAt the syndesmosis; oblique or spiral; syndesmosis variably injured.Stable if congruent; ORIF if talar shift.
Weber CAbove the syndesmosis; syndesmosis torn, often medial injury.Unstable; ORIF with syndesmosis fixation.

Additional classification detail

  • Danis-Weber A (infrasyndesmotic): fibula fractures below the syndesmosis; syndesmosis and deltoid intact; transverse avulsion; stable unless the medial malleolus is also fractured.
  • Danis-Weber B (transsyndesmotic): fibula fractures at the syndesmosis; oblique or spiral; syndesmosis intact or partially torn; stability variable; syndesmosis injured in up to 40%.
  • Danis-Weber C (suprasyndesmotic): fibula fractures above the syndesmosis; syndesmosis usually completely torn; often medial malleolus fracture or deltoid rupture with tibiofibular widening; always unstable.
  • Lauge-Hansen equivalents: Weber A is supination-adduction, Weber B is supination-external rotation, Weber C is pronation-external rotation; about 75 to 80% of ankle injuries are external rotation injuries.
  • Lauge-Hansen names foot position then deforming force, and stages the injury by how far the force travels. Supination-external rotation (the commonest, equals Weber B) runs in four stages: 1 anterior tibiofibular ligament; 2 spiral distal fibula fracture at the syndesmosis; 3 posterior malleolus or posterior tibiofibular ligament; 4 medial malleolus fracture or deltoid rupture. A higher stage means more structures failed and a less stable ankle.
  • Supination-adduction (equals Weber A) has two stages: 1 transverse fibula avulsion below the plafond; 2 vertical or oblique medial malleolus fracture. Pronation-external rotation (equals Weber C) progresses medial malleolus or deltoid, then anterior syndesmosis, then a high fibula fracture, then posterior structures.
  • Maisonneuve: proximal fibula fracture with medial and syndesmotic disruption; suspect when there is medial injury but no visible distal fibula fracture.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Confirm an isolated fibula fracture with a congruent mortise, no medial tenderness or swelling, and no talar shift.
  • Below-knee cast, short-leg posterior splint with the ankle at 90 degrees, or a CAM walking boot.
  • Weightbearing as tolerated for a stable congruent fracture.
  • Recheck mortise congruity at 10 to 14 days; immobilize about 6 weeks total with serial radiographs.

Operative

  • ORIF of the fibula with an anatomic plate and screws, or a lag screw for a simple oblique fracture.
  • Restore fibular length and rotation and reduce the talus under the tibia.
  • Assess the syndesmosis intraoperatively; fix with a screw or suture-button if displacement exceeds 2 mm.
  • Protected weightbearing usually around 6 to 8 weeks postoperatively.

Operative when: Talar shift on weightbearing imaging, medial clear space widening versus the opposite side, an associated medial malleolus fracture or deltoid disruption, syndesmotic instability, or a Weber C fracture.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. Reduce only for an associated dislocation or gross deformity, especially with skin tenting or neurovascular compromise; reduce before radiographs in those cases.
  2. Analgesia: procedural sedation with ketamine or propofol, or an intra-articular hematoma block with about 10 mL of 1% lidocaine.
  3. Position supine and flex the knee to relax the gastrocnemius.
  4. Grasp the heel with one hand and the dorsal metatarsals with the other, then apply longitudinal traction.
  5. For a posterior dislocation, plantarflex slightly to disengage the talus, then pull the foot forward while an assistant counterpresses the distal tibia and fibula.
  6. Recheck pulses, capillary refill, toe movement, and sensation, then obtain post-reduction radiographs.

Immobilization options
  • Below-knee posterior backslab: acute, non-weightbearing; accommodates swelling
  • Walking boot (CAM): stable isolated Weber A or B, weightbearing as tolerated
  • Below-knee cast: definitive immobilization once swelling settles
finished below-knee posterior backslab

ankle neutral at 90 deg dorsiflexion, knee free; add a U-shaped stirrup for unstable injuries

  1. Position the ankle neutral at 90 degrees of dorsiflexion.
  2. Apply stockinette and padding over the malleoli and heel.
  3. Lay the posterior slab from below the fibular head to the metatarsal heads, covering the heel.
  4. Add a U-shaped stirrup under the heel and up both sides to block inversion and eversion for unstable fractures.
  5. Wrap, mould at 90 degrees, then recheck distal pulses and sensation.

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • isolated lateral malleolus fracture
  • congruent mortise
  • absence of medial tenderness
  • absence of talar shift
  • intact neurovascular exam
  • immobilized in a below-knee cast or boot
  • able to mobilize safely
Refer / transfer now if
  • talar shift
  • medial clear space widening
  • associated medial malleolus fracture
  • associated deltoid injury
  • syndesmotic instability
  • Weber C fracture
  • irreducible dislocation
  • open fracture
  • neurovascular compromise
  • unstable, displaced, or dislocated fractures
Follow-up
  • stable non-displaced malleolar fractures to orthopedics in 5 to 7 days
  • stable fractures to fracture clinic or primary care
  • repeat radiographs in 7 to 10 days
  • mortise reassessment at 10 to 14 days

Key numbers

Pitfalls

  • A Salter-Harris I distal fibula fracture in a child is missed and called a sprain when tenderness is over the malleolus.
  • Medial tenderness or swelling with an isolated fibula fracture signals deltoid injury and an unstable bimalleolar-equivalent fracture.
  • A mortise that looks congruent without weightbearing can shift on a stress or weightbearing view.
  • Not palpating the proximal fibula misses a Maisonneuve injury.
  • A posterior malleolus or tertius fragment can be the only clue to an unstable rotational injury and is easy to miss on plain films.
