DefinitionFracture at the proximal articular surface of the radius within the elbow joint.

MechanismCaused by fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH) in pronation leading to the radial head being driven into the capitellum.

Diagnosis

  • Pain in the lateral elbow, especially with pronation/supination of forearm.
  • Swelling laterally and tenderness of radial head.
  • Pain with flexion/extension of the elbow.
  • Inability or difficulty with pronation or supination at the elbow.
  • Associated injuries are common, including capitellum fracture, olecranon fracture, coronoid fracture, medial collateral ligament injury, elbow dislocation, distal radioulnar joint injury, and interosseous membrane disruption.
  • Essex-Lopresti injury is a radial head fracture plus interosseous membrane disruption plus distal radioulnar joint injury, which produces longitudinal instability of the forearm.
  • The terrible triad is an elbow dislocation plus a radial head fracture plus a coronoid process fracture.
  • Palpate the whole forearm and the wrist. Tenderness at the distal radioulnar joint or along the forearm raises concern for an Essex-Lopresti injury.
  • Typically diagnosed on elbow X-ray.
  • Look for abnormal fat pad signs.
  • Look for radiocapitellar line disruption.

Imaging

ViewsAP, lateral, Greenspan

  • keySail sign: Elevated, sharply demarcated anterior fat pad
  • Any visualized posterior fat pad (highly specific for intra-articular fracture)
  • Cortical disruption, step-off, or angulation of the radial head/neck

Key radiographic lines

  • Radiocapitellar line

Classification

Mason classification

TypeCharacteristicsTreatment
Mason INondisplaced or minimally displaced (<2mm) with no mechanical block to rotation, supination and pronation intact or only limited by pain, and no comminution.Sling placement and early mobilization.
Mason IIDisplaced fracture >2mm or angulated, with possible mechanical block to forearm rotation due to the fracture fragment, and minimal comminution.Immobilization with posterior long arm splint in flexion and operative follow-up for ORIF, fragment excision, or radial head replacement.
Mason IIIComminuted and displaced fracture causing mechanical disruption to rotational motion, with complete fragments.Immobilization with posterior long arm splint in flexion and operative follow-up for ORIF, fragment excision, or radial head replacement.
Mason IVRadial head fracture with associated elbow dislocation.Immediate closed reduction of the elbow dislocation in the ED, then urgent coordination for ORIF or radial head arthroplasty due to need for annular ligamentous repair, with immobilization in a posterior long arm splint in flexion and operative follow-up.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Acute pain management.
  • Ice, elevation.
  • Sling placement.
  • Early mobilization (for Mason Type I).
  • Consider risk for compartment syndrome.
  • Neurovascular compromise from fracture requires emergent reduction and/or orthopedic intervention.
  • Open fractures require immediate IV antibiotics and urgent surgical washout.

Operative

  • ORIF (Open Reduction Internal Fixation).
  • Fragment excision.
  • Radial head replacement or arthroplasty.

Operative when: Radial head fractures with mechanical block (Mason Type II), multiple comminuted fractures (Mason Type III), or associated elbow dislocation (Mason Type IV) should have urgent orthopaedic consultation for possible ORIF, fragment excision, or radial head replacement.

Key numbers

Most common elbow injury among adults (50% among adults, 20% overall).Up to 12% of elbow fractures were missed on initial radiographic interpretation in the ED.Nearly half (45%) of all cases demonstrated inaccurate fragment number assessment on radiographs when compared to CT scan.

Pitfalls

  • Radial head fractures are one of the most commonly missed injuries in the ED and on radiographs.
  • Radiographs show poor inter- and intra-observer reliability for determining radial head fracture morphology.
  • Fracture displacement was generally underestimated on radiographic measurements when compared to CT scan.
  • High-resolution CT should be considered for patients with Mason II radial head fractures, especially in cases of poorly visualized fracture characteristics or borderline amounts of displacement.
  • Aspirate the hemarthrosis and inject lidocaine, then retest passive pronation and supination: persistent loss after anesthesia is a true mechanical block needing surgery, whereas pain-only restriction that resolves is treated nonoperatively with early motion.
  • Do not excise the radial head when the interosseous membrane is disrupted (Essex-Lopresti): the radial head is the primary longitudinal stabilizer, so excision causes proximal radial migration and ulnocarpal impaction; replace it instead.
