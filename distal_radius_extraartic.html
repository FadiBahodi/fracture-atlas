DefinitionA distal radius fracture occurs when the radius, one of the two long bones in the forearm, breaks close to the wrist.

MechanismThe most common cause is a fall onto an outstretched arm. High-energy accidents like falls from ladders or car crashes can cause these fractures in young people. In older people, especially with osteoporosis, a simple fall can cause a distal radius fracture.

Diagnosis

  • Immediate pain, tenderness, bruising, and swelling of the wrist after a fall onto an outstretched hand.
  • Visible deformity in displaced fractures; the dorsally displaced pattern gives a dinner-fork (silver-fork) profile.
  • Document median nerve function before and after reduction: numbness or paraesthesia in the radial three-and-a-half digits signals acute median nerve compression (acute carpal tunnel syndrome), the limb threat of this fracture.
  • Worsening median symptoms, especially with marked displacement or a tense volar swelling, are an emergency, not an expected bruise.
  • Confirm with PA and lateral radiographs; two orthogonal views suffice.

Criteria normal vs acceptable vs refer

Acute carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve compression)wikem, orthobullets, statpearls/ncbi
  • Document median nerve function before and after reduction: sensation in the radial three-and-a-half digits and thumb abduction (abductor pollicis brevis).
  • A displaced fracture can compress the median nerve from fragment pressure, haematoma, and traction; the distal radius fracture is the commonest cause of acute carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • Worsening numbness or pain after reduction, especially with an over-flexed splint, is a red flag, not an expected bruise.
  • First step is prompt reduction to offload the nerve, then reassess: many settle once length and tilt are restored. Avoid the extreme-flexion Cotton-Loder position.
  • Numbness that persists or progresses after reduction is an emergency and needs urgent hand or orthopaedic referral for carpal tunnel release.
Unstable / likely to redisplaceorthobullets, radiopaedia, statpearls/ncbi
  • Predictors of lost reduction (Lafontaine): dorsal angulation over 20 degrees, dorsal comminution, intra-articular extension, associated ulnar fracture, age over 60; three or more predicts redisplacement. Revised criteria add initial radial shortening over 3 mm.
  • Marked initial shortening, dorsal comminution, and osteoporotic bone redisplace even after a good reduction; repeat the film at 1 week.
  • Articular step-off does not apply to a true extra-articular fracture; any step-off reclassifies it as intra-articular and is itself a surgical indication (step-off or gap 2 mm or more under 65).

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Pain control for immediate pain and tenderness
  • Immobilization with a splint for stable fractures
  • Closed reduction for displaced fractures

Operative

  • Volar locking plate fixation

Operative when: Distal radius fractures with articular involvement, open fractures, comminuted fractures, and displaced fractures are more difficult to treat and more likely to require surgical management.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. Anaesthesia: hematoma block; use procedural sedation or a Bier block if the block is inadequate or the fracture is markedly displaced.
  2. Hematoma block: 23 to 25 gauge needle into the fracture haematoma from the dorsal side, aspirate a blood flashback, then inject 5 to 10 mL of 1 percent lidocaine without adrenaline (lower volume in low-weight or elderly patients; max about 4.5 mg/kg).
  3. Wait 10 to 15 minutes for the block to take.
  4. Finger traps on the thumb and index, elbow at 90 degrees, hang about 10 lb (5 kg) of countertraction over several minutes to disimpact the fragments.
  5. Recreate the deformity first to unhook the impacted fragment, then reverse it.
  6. Dorsally angulated (Colles-type, the common pattern): apply steady longitudinal traction, press the distal fragment from dorsal to volar to restore volar tilt, then settle the wrist into slight flexion and ulnar deviation.
  7. Volarly angulated (Smith-type): reverse the manoeuvre, translating the distal fragment dorsally and holding the wrist in slight extension.
  8. Maintain traction and a three-point mould while the plaster sets.
  9. Confirm radial length, radial inclination, and restored volar tilt on post-reduction films before completing the splint.
Splint / castsugar-tong
Immobilization options
  • Sugar-tong splint: acute displaced fracture; accommodates swelling and blocks pronation-supination
  • Volar (short-arm) splint: stable, minimally displaced extra-articular fracture
  • Short-arm cast: stable fracture once swelling settles

elbow 90 deg, forearm slight pronation, wrist neutral to slight flexion; controls forearm rotation and accommodates swelling

  1. Sugar-tong for the acute injury: it spans the wrist and blocks forearm rotation while leaving room for swelling; a volar slab is an alternative for stable, minimally displaced fractures.
  2. Elbow at 90 degrees, forearm in slight pronation.
  3. Hold the reduction with the wrist in slight palmar flexion (about 15 degrees) and slight ulnar deviation (about 20 degrees) for a dorsally displaced fracture; aim for slight, not extreme, positions.
  4. Apply stockinette and padding, then lay one continuous plaster slab from the dorsal MCP heads, around the elbow, back to the volar MCP heads.
  5. Set a three-point mould: pressure dorsal over the distal fragment and volar above and below it, to resist dorsal redisplacement.
  6. Leave the MCP joints, fingers, and thumb free so the patient can flex them.
  7. Do not over-flex the wrist: the extreme-flexion Cotton-Loder position raises carpal tunnel pressure and can cause acute median nerve compression.
  8. Do not apply a circumferential cast on a freshly swollen wrist; split the cast or use a slab to allow for swelling, then recheck distal circulation and median nerve function.

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • non-displaced
  • stable extra-articular fracture
  • appropriate immobilization
  • adequate pain control
Refer / transfer now if
  • displaced or angulated beyond acceptable parameters after attempted reduction
  • dorsal angulation over 20 degrees
  • radial shortening over 5 mm
  • intra-articular extension
  • marked dorsal comminution
  • open fracture
  • irreducible fracture
  • median nerve symptoms that persist after reduction
Follow-up
  • ortho or fracture clinic within 72 hours to 1 week
  • repeat imaging at 1 week to check for loss of reduction
  • immobilize about 6 weeks total
  • osteoporosis workup for fragility fractures

Pitfalls

  • Acute median nerve compression after the injury or from an over-flexed splint; document nerve function before and after reduction.
  • Loss of reduction in osteoporotic and comminuted fractures; repeat the film at 1 week.
  • Associated injuries like triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) tears or carpal bone injuries may be present.
  • Ulnar styloid fracture is common and flags possible TFCC and distal radioulnar joint injury.
  • Colles is the dorsally angulated (dorsal tilt) extra-articular pattern; Smith is the volar-angulated reverse and reverses the direction of reduction.
  • Smith (volar-angulated) fractures are inherently unstable, redisplace in plaster, and usually need operative fixation; refer all Smith fractures even after a successful reduction.
  • Extensor pollicis longus rupture is a late complication, about 3 to 12 weeks out and classically after a minimally displaced fracture; counsel return for inability to lift the thumb off a flat surface.
  • In low-demand patients over 65, nonoperative management gives good function even with some malunion; radiographic parameters are relaxed and displacement alone rarely needs surgery.
