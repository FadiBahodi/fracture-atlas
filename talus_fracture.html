DefinitionThe talus is a bone in the foot that helps make up the ankle joint, connecting the leg and foot, and a talus fracture is a break in this bone.

MechanismTalus fractures are usually due to high-energy trauma, such as motor vehicle collisions or falls from a substantial height. Lateral process fractures can occur when the foot is forced outward, commonly seen in snowboarding injuries.

Diagnosis

  • Acute pain, swelling, bruising, and tenderness.
  • Inability to walk or bear weight on the foot.
  • Cuts on the skin.
  • Difficulty moving toes or ankle.
  • Decreased sensitivity on the bottom of the foot.
  • Diminished pulse in the foot.
  • Confirmed by radiographs and CT scans.

Classification

Hawkins classification

TypeCharacteristicsTreatment
Hawkins INon-displaced fractureNon-weight-bearing short-leg cast 8 to 12 weeks, or percutaneous screw fixation. Avascular necrosis about 0 to 15%.
Hawkins IIDisplaced fracture with subtalar subluxation/dislocationUrgent anatomic reduction and ORIF. Avascular necrosis about 20 to 50%.
Hawkins IIIDisplaced fracture with subtalar and ankle subluxation/dislocationEmergent reduction (often open) and ORIF; frequently open or skin-tenting. Avascular necrosis about 50 to 90%.
Hawkins IVDisplaced fracture with subtalar, ankle, and talonavicular subluxation/dislocationEmergent ORIF. Avascular necrosis about 70 to 100%.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Splint or cast immobilization.
  • No weight-bearing on the foot.
  • Used for stable, well-positioned fractures.

Operative

  • Surgical fixation with plates, screws, or rods.
  • External fixator or pins may be used initially for severe displacement.
  • Joint fusion or ankle replacement for severely damaged or missing talus.

Operative when: Most talus fractures require surgery, especially if bones are displaced or unstable.

Immobilization options
  • splint: initial immobilization for pain
  • cast: definitive treatment for stable, well-positioned fractures
  • splint or cast: post-operative immobilization
finished below-knee posterior backslab

ankle neutral at 90 deg dorsiflexion, knee free; add a U-shaped stirrup for unstable injuries

  1. Position the ankle neutral at 90 degrees of dorsiflexion.
  2. Apply stockinette and padding over the malleoli and heel, with extra over bony prominences.
  3. Lay the posterior slab from below the fibular head to the metatarsal heads, covering the heel.
  4. For an unstable injury, add a U-shaped stirrup slab around both malleoli.
  5. Mould around the malleoli and arch and wrap; leave the toes and knee free.
  6. Confirm the ankle holds 90 degrees and recheck distal pulses and sensation before the plaster sets.

Key numbers

Hawkins classification (talar neck) predicts avascular necrosis by the number of disrupted joints: I non-displaced, II subtalar, III subtalar and ankle, IV plus talonavicular.Account for 3 to 6% of all foot fractures.Account for 0.85 to less than 1% of all body fractures.Bone healing takes 8 to 12 weeks.Hawkins sign: subchondral lucency of the talar dome at 6 to 8 weeks indicates preserved vascularity and a lower avascular necrosis risk; its absence suggests avascular necrosis.

Pitfalls

  • Damage to the blood supply can lead to avascular necrosis.
  • Incorrect diagnosis or treatment can lead to pseudoarthrosis, early osteoarthritis, and ankle instability.
  • Putting weight on the foot too early can cause fracture displacement.
  • Cartilage injury can lead to arthritis.
  • Small or incomplete fractures may be missed on initial X-rays.
  • Increasing pressure from fluid buildup in the foot requires immediate action.
  • The talus has no muscular attachments and a largely retrograde blood supply: the artery of the tarsal canal enters the neck distally and flows back into the body, so a displaced neck fracture strips inflow and infarcts the proximal body; the deltoid branch is often the only remaining supply, so avoid medial soft-tissue stripping.
  • The emergency is urgent reduction of any subtalar or ankle dislocation to relieve skin tenting and vascular compromise; definitive ORIF can wait for swelling to settle without raising avascular necrosis, which correlates with neck comminution and open fracture rather than surgical delay.
