MCP joint dislocation

Hand & Fingers

ED role
Identify whether the dislocation is simple or complex, because the reduction technique differs and the wrong technique converts a simple to an irreducible complex dislocation. Look for palmar skin dimpling, the sign of a complex dislocation. Assess digital nerve function. Obtain a true lateral of the injured digit, and post-reduction films to confirm congruency. An associated metacarpal or proximal phalanx fracture is common.
Hallmark
Dorsal prominence of the proximal phalanx on the metacarpal head, with palmar skin dimpling in a complex dislocation.

DefinitionDisplacement of the metacarpophalangeal joint, most often dorsal, where the proximal phalanx displaces dorsally on the metacarpal head. The index finger and thumb are most commonly involved.

Diagnosis

  • Pain and deformity at the metacarpophalangeal joint.
  • A simple dislocation holds the finger hyperextended. A complex dislocation holds the finger in slight extension with only mild deformity and is the more deceptive presentation.
  • Palmar skin dimpling at the base of the finger indicates a complex dislocation.
  • Loss of joint space or overlap of the metacarpal head and proximal phalanx on the posteroanterior radiograph.
  • Assess digital nerve sensation distal to the injury.

Anatomy

MCP Joint Anatomy

StructureDescriptionClinical Relevance
Proper Collateral LigamentsOriginate from small depressions along either side of the metacarpal head, course obliquely to insert at the palmar aspect of the proximal phalanx.Taut in flexion, relaxed in extension. Passively restrain joint motion.
Accessory Collateral LigamentsArise at the same depression as proper collateral ligaments but in a more palmar location, extend in a palmar direction to attach firmly to the volar plate.Taut in extension, more relaxed in flexion.
Volar PlateA fibrous structure firmly attached to the accessory collateral ligaments.Can become interposed in complex dorsal dislocations, preventing closed reduction.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Closed reduction for a simple dislocation, then a dorsal extension-block splint with the MCP joint in about 30 degrees of flexion, allowing early protected motion.
  • Buddy taping and early motion once stable.

Operative

  • Open reduction for a complex dislocation with an interposed volar plate, a volar dislocation, an irreducible joint, or persistent incongruency.
  • Fixation of an associated displaced metacarpal head or proximal phalangeal base fracture.

Operative when: An irreducible or complex dislocation, an incongruent joint after closed reduction, a volar dislocation, an open injury, or an associated displaced intra-articular fracture.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. Anesthetize with a wrist or local block.
  2. Flex the wrist to relax the long flexor tendons.
  3. Hyperextend the proximal phalanx on the metacarpal to about 90 degrees to maintain contact between the bases.
  4. Apply volarly-directed pressure to the dorsal base of the proximal phalanx and translate it distally and volarly over the metacarpal head. Do not pull longitudinal traction.
  5. Confirm a smooth, congruent reduction with active range of motion and post-reduction radiographs.
  6. If reduction fails or the joint is incongruent, the dislocation is complex and needs open reduction.
Splint / castdorsal extension-block splint
Immobilization options
  • dorsal extension-block splint with the MCP joint in about 30 degrees of flexion: stable simple dislocation after reduction

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • reduced
  • congruent joint
  • stable simple dislocation
  • intact digital nerves
  • dorsal extension-block splint
Refer / transfer now if
  • irreducible dislocation
  • complex dislocation
  • palmar skin dimpling
  • volar dislocation
  • open injury
  • incongruent joint after reduction
Follow-up
  • hand surgery or hand therapy in about 1 week
  • early protected motion to prevent stiffness

Key numbers

The index finger and thumb are the most commonly dislocated metacarpophalangeal joints.A simple dislocation is held in about 60 to 90 degrees of hyperextension.The dorsal extension-block splint holds the MCP joint in about 30 degrees of flexion.

Pitfalls

  • Palmar skin dimpling indicates a complex dislocation that needs open reduction, not repeated closed attempts.
  • Longitudinal traction on a simple dislocation entraps the volar plate and converts it into an irreducible complex dislocation.
  • A complex dislocation is deceptive because it sits in near-extension with only mild deformity.
  • An incongruent or subluxed joint after reduction signals interposed soft tissue or gross instability and needs open reduction.
  • A standard whole-hand lateral obscures the joint. Get a true lateral of the injured digit.
