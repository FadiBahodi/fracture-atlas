DefinitionExtra-articular distal radial metaphyseal fracture with dorsal angulation and dorsal displacement, often radial shortening plus ulnar styloid fracture.

MechanismFOOSH onto a dorsiflexed, pronated wrist.

Diagnosis

  • FOOSH with wrist pain and swelling.
  • Dorsally angulated dinner-fork (silver-fork) deformity.
  • Tenderness over the distal radius.
  • Document median nerve function before and after reduction: radial three-and-a-half digit numbness or paraesthesia signals acute median nerve compression (acute carpal tunnel syndrome), the limb threat of this fracture.
  • Progressive or severe median symptoms, especially with marked displacement or a tense volar swelling, are an emergency, not an expected bruise.
  • AP plus lateral radiographs confirm; two orthogonal views suffice.

Imaging

ViewsAP, lateral

  • keyDistal radius metaphyseal fracture with dorsal tilt and displacement
  • Radial shortening and loss of ulnar inclination
  • Ulnar styloid fracture (present in >50%)
  • Dorsal comminution
  • Check for intra-articular step-off or gap on CT; a true Colles is extra-articular, so step-off reclassifies it as an intra-articular distal radius fracture CT

Criteria normal vs acceptable vs refer

Acute carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve compression)wikem, orthobullets
  • Acute median nerve compression complicates a displaced distal radius fracture, from fragment pressure, haematoma, and traction.
  • Test sensation in the radial three-and-a-half digits and thumb opposition before and after reduction; worsening numbness or pain is the warning sign.
  • First step is prompt fracture reduction to take pressure off the nerve, then reassess: many resolve once length and tilt are restored.
  • Avoid the Cotton-Loder position (extreme wrist flexion and ulnar deviation) in the splint, which itself causes median compression.
  • Symptoms that persist or progress after reduction need urgent hand or orthopaedic referral for carpal tunnel release; do not wait.

Additional classification detail

  • These named distal radius eponyms are distinct fracture patterns, not subtypes of Colles: a Colles is one dorsally displaced extra-articular pattern, and the table below compares the sibling eponyms (Smith, Barton, Chauffeur).
  • Colles: dorsally displaced extra-articular distal radius.
  • Smith: volarly displaced extra-articular distal radius.
  • Barton: intra-articular fracture-dislocation of the dorsal or volar lip.
  • Chauffeur (Hutchinson): intra-articular radial styloid fracture.
  • Die-punch: depressed lunate fossa fracture.
  • Frykman I: extra-articular.
  • Frykman II: extra-articular plus ulnar styloid.
  • Frykman III: radiocarpal joint involved.
  • Frykman IV: radiocarpal plus ulnar styloid.
  • Frykman V: distal radioulnar joint involved.
  • Frykman VI: DRUJ plus ulnar styloid.
  • Frykman VII: radiocarpal and DRUJ involved.
  • Frykman VIII: both joints plus ulnar styloid.
  • AO/OTA: A extra-articular, B partial articular, C complete articular.
  • Lafontaine instability: dorsal angulation over 20 deg, dorsal comminution over 50 percent, radial shortening over 5 mm, intra-articular displacement over 2 mm, age over 60; 3 or more predicts loss of reduction.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Undisplaced and within acceptable parameters: below-elbow cast or splint, no reduction.
  • Displaced but acceptable after closed reduction: cast or splint.
  • Three-point mould maintains volar tilt and resists dorsal redisplacement.
  • Immobilise about 6 weeks; repeat film at 1 week.

Operative

  • Closed reduction percutaneous K-wire pinning: extra-articular with stable volar cortex (82-90 percent good results).
  • ORIF with volar locking plate: displaced intra-articular and unstable patterns.
  • External fixation +/- pinning: severely comminuted fractures.

Operative when: Irreducible, loss of reduction, articular step-off or gap 2 mm or more, shortening over 5 mm (over 3 mm AAOS under 65), dorsal tilt over 10-20 deg, open fracture, or neurovascular compromise.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. Hematoma block: 23-25 gauge needle dorsally into fracture, aspirate blood flashback.
  2. Inject 5 to 10 mL of 1 percent lidocaine (use the lower volume in low-weight or elderly patients; max 3 mg/kg) without adrenaline (max 3 mg/kg).
  3. Alternatives: 5 mL of 2 percent lidocaine (100 mg), or lidocaine plus 5 mL 0.25 percent bupivacaine.
  4. Procedural sedation or Bier block if hematoma block inadequate.
  5. Wait 10-15 minutes for the block.
  6. Finger traps, elbow at 90 degrees, hang ~10 lb (5 kg) countertraction for several minutes.
  7. Recreate deformity: extend wrist to ~90 degrees to unhook the fragment.
  8. Pull distal, translate dorsal then volar with thumb pressure, flex and ulnar-deviate the wrist.
  9. Mould three-point plaster; confirm radial length, inclination 15-30 deg, volar tilt toward normal, about 11 degrees (neutral to slight volar) on post-reduction films.
Splint / castsugar-tong
Immobilization options
  • Sugar-tong splint: acute; accommodates swelling and blocks pronation-supination
  • Short-arm cast: stable, extra-articular, once swelling settles

elbow 90 deg, forearm slight pronation, wrist neutral to slight flexion; controls forearm rotation and accommodates swelling

  1. Elbow at 90 degrees, forearm in slight pronation.
  2. Hold the reduction with the wrist in slight palmar flexion (about 15 degrees) or neutral and slight ulnar deviation (about 20 degrees).
  3. Do not splint in wrist extension; extension reproduces the dorsal displacement and loses the reduction.
  4. Stockinette and padding, then plaster volar forearm, around elbow, dorsal forearm to the MCP joints.
  5. Leave MCP joints and thumb free.
  6. Avoid the extreme-flexion Cotton-Loder position (marked palmar flexion plus ulnar deviation): it raises carpal tunnel pressure and risks acute median nerve compression. Target slight flexion or neutral, not extreme flexion.

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • stable or reduced to acceptable alignment
  • sugar-tong splint applied
  • neurovascularly intact
  • pain controlled
Refer / transfer now if
  • dorsal angulation > 20 degrees
  • intra-articular extension
  • marked comminution
  • displacement > two-thirds width
  • shortening > 5 mm
  • open fracture
  • neurovascular compromise
  • irreducible fracture
Follow-up
  • ortho or fracture clinic within 72 hours to 1 week
  • repeat imaging at 1 week
  • cast immobilization for ~6 weeks total
  • osteoporosis workup for fragility fractures

Pitfalls

  • Acute median nerve compression after the injury or an over-flexed Cotton-Loder cast; document nerve before and after reduction.
  • Late extensor pollicis longus rupture, more common after non-displaced fractures, presenting weeks later.
  • Loss of reduction in osteoporotic and comminuted fractures; repeat film at 1 week.
  • Associated ulnar styloid fracture in over 50-60 percent flags TFCC and DRUJ injury.
  • Volar angulation means a Smith fracture, which reverses reduction direction.
  • Malunion gives a fixed dinner-fork deformity; articular incongruity predisposes to osteoarthritis.
  • In low-demand patients over 65, radiographic malunion correlates poorly with function and operative fixation gives no long-term benefit over casting; about a third heal in malunion with good function, so imperfect alignment is often accepted rather than referred for surgery.
  • Scrutinize the carpus on the AP: a scapholunate gap over 3 mm suggests scapholunate dissociation, and check the snuffbox for a concurrent scaphoid fracture.
