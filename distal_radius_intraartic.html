Distal radius fracture (intra-articular)

Wrist & Distal Forearm
radiograph

ED role
  • Screen median nerve function before and after any reduction with sensation in the radial three-and-a-half digits as the primary test; thumb opposition adds motor information but is often limited by acute pain.
  • Numbness that is new or progressive after reduction is the warning for acute carpal tunnel syndrome and prompts urgent decompression.
  • Assess for open injury and compartment syndrome of the forearm.
  • Reduce most angulated or displaced fractures closed under hematoma block or procedural sedation and apply a sugar-tong splint, even when operative fixation is expected, to control pain and swelling.
  • Obtain post-reduction PA and lateral films.
  • Refer intra-articular fractures with step-off, comminution, or distal radioulnar joint instability to orthopedics, because most require operative fixation.
Hallmark
Fracture line reaching the articular surface on the PA or lateral view, often with a radial styloid fragment or a depressed lunate fossa fragment. Deformity varies with the pattern: a dinner-fork shape with dorsal displacement, a garden-spade shape with volar (Barton) displacement, or minimal deformity in a radial styloid (Chauffeur) fracture.

DefinitionA distal radius fracture whose fracture line extends into the radiocarpal or distal radioulnar articular surface. Articular involvement may be partial (AO type B) or complete (AO type C).

MechanismFall onto an outstretched hand is the most common cause. Younger patients sustain these from high-energy injury such as a fall from height or a motor vehicle or bicycle crash; older patients with osteoporosis sustain them from a standing-height fall. Axial load through the carpus drives the lunate or scaphoid fossa into the articular surface.

Diagnosis

  • Wrist or forearm pain and swelling after a fall onto an outstretched hand.
  • Dinner-fork deformity of the wrist.
  • Tenderness, ecchymosis, and crepitus over the distal radius.
  • Numbness in the radial three-and-a-half digits indicates median nerve involvement and needs to be addressed quickly to prevent permanent damage.
  • PA and lateral radiographs are the mainstay of diagnosis.
  • CT improves assessment of articular step-off, gap, and fracture pattern when plain films are insufficient.
  • MRI is used when ligamentous or triangular fibrocartilage complex injury is suspected or a fracture is suspected but not seen on radiographs.

Criteria normal vs acceptable vs refer

Acute carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve compression)orthoinfo
  • Acute median nerve compression complicates a displaced intra-articular distal radius fracture from fragment pressure, hematoma, and traction.
  • Test sensation in the radial three-and-a-half digits and thumb opposition before and after reduction; worsening numbness or pain is the warning sign.
  • Reduce the fracture promptly to take pressure off the nerve, then reassess.
  • Symptoms that persist or progress after reduction need urgent hand or orthopedic referral for carpal tunnel release.

Additional classification detail

  • These named eponyms are the intra-articular distal radius patterns, distinct fracture types rather than subtypes of one another.
  • Barton: intra-articular fracture-dislocation of the radiocarpal joint involving the volar or dorsal lip, with the carpus displaced with the fragment; the volar rim fragment anchors carpal stability, so it is unstable and needs buttress fixation.
  • A Barton fracture may involve the volar or the dorsal rim; the volar variant is sometimes labelled a reverse Barton and often needs volar buttress plating.
  • Chauffeur: intra-articular fracture of the radial styloid, also called a Hutchinson fracture.
  • Die-punch: depressed fracture of the lunate fossa articular surface.
  • AO/OTA: type A extra-articular, type B partial articular, type C complete articular; the intra-articular distal radius is type B or type C.
  • Frykman uses intra-articular involvement of the radiocarpal and distal radioulnar joints plus the presence of an ulnar styloid fracture; odd numbers have no ulnar styloid fracture and even numbers add an ulnar styloid fracture.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Closed reduction and a sugar-tong splint for fractures reduced to acceptable articular and radiographic alignment.
  • Sugar-tong splint with the elbow at 90 degrees and the forearm in slight pronation; position the wrist in neutral for most patterns.
  • Avoid wrist flexion in volar-rim (volar Barton) and volar-displaced patterns, where flexion worsens displacement; splint these in slight extension and supination.
  • Immobilization alone for nondisplaced articular fractures within acceptable parameters.
  • Repeat film at 1 week to detect loss of reduction.
  • Counsel about late extensor pollicis longus rupture, which can present weeks after a non-displaced or minimally displaced fracture.

Operative

  • Open reduction and internal fixation with a volar locking plate.
  • Fragment-specific or volar plating for displaced articular fragments.
  • Bridging external fixation for highly comminuted patterns.
  • Dorsal bridging plating as an alternative for comminuted articular fractures.

Operative when: Intra-articular fractures with articular involvement are more likely to require surgery; operative fixation is recommended for post-reduction radial shortening greater than 3 mm, dorsal tilt greater than 10 degrees, intra-articular displacement or step-off of 2 mm or more, irreducible fractures, open fractures, distal radioulnar joint instability, or neurovascular compromise.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. Provide analgesia with a hematoma block or procedural sedation; procedural sedation is preferred when the block is inadequate.
  2. Apply axial traction with finger traps and hanging weights if available.
  3. Recreate then reverse the mechanism of injury to free periosteum folded into the fracture site.
  4. Maintain axial traction throughout the maneuver.
  5. For dorsally angulated fragments, exaggerate dorsal angulation under traction, then reduce the distal fragment volarly and back to length.
  6. Apply a sugar-tong splint and re-image.
  7. Target anatomic articular reduction; refer intra-articular fractures that do not meet acceptable parameters.
Splint / castsugar-tong
Immobilization options
  • Sugar-tong splint: after reduction; accommodates swelling and blocks pronation-supination
  • Volar short-arm splint: reduction not attempted or failed; convert to sugar-tong if pronation or supination is painful

elbow 90 deg, forearm slight pronation, wrist neutral to slight flexion; controls forearm rotation and accommodates swelling

  1. Hold the elbow at 90 degrees with the forearm in slight pronation and the wrist in neutral.
  2. Apply stockinette and padding from the dorsal MCPs, around the elbow, to the volar MCPs.
  3. Lay one continuous slab: volar forearm, around the elbow at the olecranon, then back along the dorsal forearm to the MCP joints.
  4. Mould the U-shaped slab so it blocks pronation and supination.
  5. Splint volar-rim (volar Barton) and volar-displaced patterns in slight extension and supination; avoid wrist flexion, which worsens displacement, and let the consulting orthopedist direct positioning for unstable rim fractures.
  6. Avoid the Cotton-Loder position of extreme wrist flexion and ulnar deviation, which itself causes acute median nerve compression.
  7. Leave the MCP joints, fingers, and thumb free.
  8. Recheck distal circulation before the plaster sets.

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • stable or reduced to acceptable alignment
  • immobilized in a sugar-tong splint
  • intact neurovascular exam
  • controlled pain
  • orthopedic follow-up arranged
Refer / transfer now if
  • open fracture
  • neurovascular compromise
  • acute median nerve compression
  • compartment concern
  • irreducible fracture
  • unacceptable radial shortening, tilt, or inclination
  • articular step-off of 2 mm or more
  • distal radioulnar joint instability
Follow-up
  • orthopedics or fracture clinic within 1 week
  • repeat PA and lateral films to assess for loss of reduction
  • maintain splint immobilization until orthopedic review

Pitfalls

  • Missing acute median nerve compression; document nerve function before and after reduction and reduce promptly when it is present.
  • Splinting in the Cotton-Loder position of extreme flexion and ulnar deviation, which itself causes acute median nerve compression.
  • Reading an articular step-off as acceptable: a step-off of 2 mm or more predicts post-traumatic arthritis and is an operative trigger.
  • Plain radiographs alone underestimate the amount and location of intra-articular pathology; obtain CT for the articular detail.
  • Treating an intra-articular fracture as a simple Colles and missing distal radioulnar joint instability or a triangular fibrocartilage complex injury.
  • Delayed diagnosis of a distal radius fracture leads to significant morbidity.
  • Late extensor pollicis longus rupture presenting weeks after a minimally displaced fracture.
