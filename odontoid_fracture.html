Odontoid (dens) fracture

Cervical Spine

The open-mouth odontoid view serves as a rapid screening tool for suspected upper cervical injury, and any asymmetry of articular spaces should prompt considerable consideration for dens fracture.

DefinitionOdontoid fractures are fractures of the dens (odontoid process) of the axis (C2).

MechanismOdontoid fractures result from hyperextension or hyperflexion forces acting on the upper cervical spine. In younger patients, high-energy trauma is the typical cause, while in elderly patients, low-energy falls are the most common mechanism.

Diagnosis

  • Pain
  • Inability to move ranging to quadriplegia
  • Feeling of instability of head on spine
  • Midline tenderness
  • Limited motion
  • Flexion-extension injury
  • CT with sagittal and coronal reformats is the definitive imaging study

Imaging

ViewsOpen-mouth (odontoid), Lateral, CT sagittal and coronal

  • keyFracture line at the base of the dens
  • Anterior or posterior displacement of the dens relative to the C2 body
  • Widening of the predentate space >3 mm
  • Asymmetry of the articular spaces between the dens and the lateral masses of C1
  • Asymmetry of the articular spaces between the lateral masses of C1 and the body of C2

Key radiographic lines

  • Diagastric Line
  • Bimastoid Line

  • Not intoxicated
  • No distracting injury

Classification

TypeCharacteristicsTreatment
Type IOblique fracture through the upper portion of the odontoid tip at the insertion of the alar ligaments. Rarest type (<5% of odontoid fractures).Rigid cervical collar for 6 to 8 weeks if isolated. Surgical fixation if associated with craniocervical instability.
Type IIFracture at the junction of the odontoid process and the C2 vertebral body. Most common type (60 to 65% of odontoid fractures). High rate of nonunion (25 to 40%) due to limited blood supply.Minimally displaced fractures in younger patients may be treated with halo vest immobilization. Displaced fractures (>5 mm), posterior displacement, or elderly patients typically require surgical fixation (anterior odontoid screw or C1-C2 arthrodesis).
Type IIAVariant of Type II with comminution at the base of the dens. Further increases the risk of nonunion.Management similar to Type II, but anterior screw fixation is technically more difficult.
Type IIIFracture extending into the body of the axis, often involving the superior articular facets. Better prognosis and higher union rate than Type II.Not specified in sources, but noted to have best prognosis.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Rigid cervical collar for 6 to 8 weeks (Type I, if isolated)
  • Halo immobilization for 12 to 16 weeks (Type II, minimally displaced in younger patients)

Operative

  • Surgical fixation (Type I if craniocervical instability)
  • Surgical fixation (Type II, if displaced, posterior displacement, or elderly patients)
  • Anterior odontoid screw (Type II)
  • Arthrodesis of C1 to C2 (Type II)

Operative when: Surgical fixation is required for Type I fractures associated with craniocervical instability, and typically for Type II displaced fractures (>5 mm), posterior displacement, or in elderly patients.

Disposition

Refer / transfer now if
  • craniocervical instability
  • displacement greater than 5 mm
  • posterior displacement
  • elderly patient

Key numbers

10 to 15% of all cervical spine fracturesMost common fracture type at the C2 levelType I fractures: less than 5% of odontoid fracturesType II fractures: 60 to 65% of odontoid fracturesType II nonunion rate: about 30 to 40% (higher with displacement over 5 mm or age over 50).Type III fractures: 30% of dens fracturesRisk factor for nonunion: Age greater than 50 yearsRisk factor for nonunion: Displacement greater than 5 mm

Pitfalls

  • A mach line may appear to traverse the base of the dens on the open-mouth view but is recognized by the superimposed base of the occiput and absence on lateral view.
  • A smooth and sclerotic edge to a 'fracture' usually indicates congenital non-union or acquired non-union (os odontoideum).
  • Os odontoideum must be distinguished from an acute Type II odontoid fracture; os odontoideum has smooth, sclerotic margins and lacks surrounding edema on MRI, while an acute fracture has irregular margins and shows bone marrow edema on STIR.
  • Ensure no overlap of the dens by the teeth on the open-mouth view.
