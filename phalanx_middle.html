DefinitionA fracture of the middle phalanx bone in the finger.

MechanismDirect blow, axial load, blunt trauma, crush injuries, falls, or sports-related impacts can cause middle phalanx fractures.

Diagnosis

  • Visual inspection for open wounds, deformity, and rotational malalignment (scissoring on flexion).
  • Palpation for local swelling, bruising, and tenderness.
  • Assessment of passive and active range of motion.
  • Evaluation for malrotation, where flexed fingers should point towards the scaphoid bone or radial styloid.
  • X-rays of the digit (AP, lateral, oblique) confirm the fracture.
  • Test tendon integrity: FDS and FDP function, and the central slip with Elson's test (a middle phalanx fracture near the PIP can disrupt the extensor central slip).

Additional classification detail

Middle phalanx fractures can be classified by location, orientation, displacement, angulation, open vs. closed, comminution, and joint involvement. Specific patterns include Boutonnière deformity and volar plate avulsion.

Treatment

Non-operative

  • Buddy taping to an adjacent finger for 3 to 4 weeks for minimally angulated, extraarticular, and minimally displaced fractures.
  • Aluminum splint for minimally angulated, extraarticular, and minimally displaced fractures.
  • Short term immobilization (1 to 2 weeks) with a foam-laminated aluminum and fiberglass splint followed by early range of motion.
  • Dorsal extension block splinting for volar plate fractures causing joint subluxation.

Operative

  • Closed reduction with percutaneous pinning if closed reduction and splinting fails.
  • Open reduction and internal fixation if closed reduction and splinting fails.

Operative when: Surgery is indicated for open fractures, intra-articular fractures, polytrauma, unreducible fractures, nerve, vessel, or soft-tissue damage, joint surface involvement greater than 30%, inadequate fracture reduction (e.g., persistent malrotation), displaced intra-articular fractures, fracture dislocations, or fractures causing finger rotation or shortening.

PediatricIn younger patients, imaging of the contralateral hand can be done for comparison, and Salter Harris fractures (types II, III, IV) can occur.

Reduction & splinting

Reduction
  1. 1. Administer digital block or hematoma block for anesthesia.
  2. 2. Reduce by traction and manipulation of the finger.
Splint / cast
Immobilization options
  • Buddy taping: Minimally angulated, extraarticular, and minimally displaced fractures (early protected motion, typically 3 to 4 weeks).
  • Aluminum splint: Minimally angulated, extraarticular, and minimally displaced fractures; or for short term immobilization (1 to 2 weeks).
  • Dorsal aluminum splint in extension: Reserved for central slip avulsion, with the PIP held in extension to prevent boutonniere deformity. Most reduced shaft fractures need about 3 to 4 weeks of immobilization, then protected motion; unstable patterns may need longer. Volar plate injuries instead use a dorsal extension-block splint.
  • Radial gutter splint: Complicated or unstable fractures.
  • Ulnar gutter splint: Complicated or unstable fractures.
  • Dorsal extension block splint: Volar plate fractures causing joint subluxation.

Disposition

Consider discharge when
  • fracture is reduced in the ED
  • pain is adequately managed
  • wounds are cleaned and bandaged
  • antibiotics and tetanus booster given if required
  • patient education complete
Refer / transfer now if
  • open fractures
  • intra-articular fractures
  • polytrauma
  • unreducible fractures
  • nerve damage
  • vessel damage
  • soft-tissue damage
  • surgery required and cannot be performed at current facility
Follow-up
  • repeat evaluation with X-rays at 7 to 10 days to confirm alignment
  • repeat X-rays at 3 to 4 weeks
  • repeat X-rays every 2 weeks
  • anticipate 4 to 6 weeks for healing

Key numbers

Angulation depends on the fracture position relative to the flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS) insertion: a fracture proximal to the FDS insertion angulates apex dorsal, while one distal to it angulates apex volar; this determines the reduction and splint position.Phalanx fractures are among the most common hand fractures.Middle phalanx fractures are less frequent than distal phalanx fractures.The little finger is affected in nearly 40% of phalanx fracture cases.Emergency departments treat 35% of initial finger injuries.

Pitfalls

  • Failure to identify malrotation can lead to poor functional outcomes.
  • Displaced or unstable fractures can cause fingers to become stiff quickly.
  • Intraarticular phalangeal condylar fractures can result in osteonecrosis, osteoarthritis, or stiff joints.
